fundamental forces

 Fundamental forces in physics (sometimes called interaction forces) are the forces by which the simplest particles in the world interact with each other.
We call a force "fundamental" when it cannot be described by other forces. 
The four known fundamental forces are:
the weak nuclear force, the strong nuclear force, the electromagnetic force, and gravity.
Strong and weak nuclear forces are also known as strong and weak forces, respectively.
Probably, except for gravity, the rest of these forces can be described by a series of approximate calculations known as perturbation theory.

Gravity

Gravity is a natural phenomenon in which all objects with mass attract each other. The effect of gravity on these bodies, 
that is, the effect of the attraction of a massed body on another massed body, or in a simpler understanding, each body on another body; And we see it as a weight on us.

One of the most familiar manifestations of gravity is the falling of an apple from a tree or the attraction of the weight of physical objects and 
the application of downward force on them. 
The phenomenon of gravity usually occurs on large or very large scales when the mass of at least one of the bodies involved is very high; 
Therefore, the manifestations of gravity can be seen in the movement of celestial bodies and the path of the planets around the sun.

Electromagnetism

Electromagnetism is the force that acts between electrically charged particles. This phenomenon includes the electrostatic force acting between charged particles at rest, 
and the combined effect of electric and magnetic forces acting between charged particles moving relative to each other.

Electromagnetism has an infinite range, as gravity does, but is vastly stronger. It is the force that binds electrons to atoms, and it holds molecules together. 
It is responsible for everyday phenomena like light, magnets, electricity, and friction. Electromagnetism fundamentally determines all macroscopic, and many atomic-level, 
properties of the chemical elements.

The strong interaction, or strong nuclear force, is the most complex of the fundamental interactions, mostly because its value changes with distance.
